Essay Structure Guidelines

Detailed breakdown of the four-paragraph essay structure

Paragraph 1: Introduction and Overview

  • • Introduce your chosen text (author, title, publication context, main topic)
  • • Briefly explain how this text connects to your personal and academic interests
  • • State your main response to the text

Paragraph 2: Personal and Cultural Connections

  • • Connect the text's main ideas to your personal background, cultural context, or lived experiences
  • • Explain why these ideas matter to you and how they appear in or influence your own life
  • • Explain how your identity shapes your perspective of this text

Paragraph 3: Argument Analysis

  • • Identify the author's main argument or purpose
  • • Analyse specific writing strategies the author uses (evidence, appeals, structure, language choices)
  • • Comment on these strategies - what works, what doesn't, and why

Paragraph 4: Disciplinary Reflection

  • • Connect the text to your academic discipline or broad academic interests
  • • Comment on how this text might contribute to broader discussions in your field
  • • Consider implications for related academic disciplines

Gender-Based Violence in South Africa

Critical reading activity based on the article "Sweden on the road: Empowering South Africa's next generation"

Article Summary

The article discusses a collaborative initiative between Sweden and South Africa to empower young leaders in the fight against Gender-Based Violence (GBV). The "Sweden on the Road" Women's Month edition brought together students, youth activists, academics, policymakers, and civil society organizations to address GBV challenges and develop youth-centered solutions.

"Young people are not just the future, they are the present. Their courage, energy and creativity are what will move societies forward." - Elisabeth Hårleman, Swedish Embassy

Analytical Questions

1. What types of evidence does the author use to support the claim that youth leadership is central to addressing gender-based violence? Consider whether the evidence is statistical, anecdotal, institutional (e.g., referencing the National Strategic Plan), or testimonial (e.g., quotes from officials). How does this shape the credibility of the argument?

2. How does the author construct the identities of South African youth and Swedish partners, and what persuasive purpose does this serve? Look at how young people are described (e.g., "courage, energy, creativity") and how Sweden is positioned as a supportive ally. What assumptions or values are embedded in these portrayals?

3. In what ways does the article appeal to cultural values or shared histories between South Africa and Sweden? Identify references to "historic partnership," "shared commitment," or "global advocacy." How do these cultural and diplomatic appeals reinforce the message and invite reader alignment?

4. Which emotional appeals are used to engage the reader, and how do they function within the broader argument? Pay attention to language that evokes hope, urgency, or pride—especially in phrases like "empowering young leaders" or "solutions to South Africa's toughest challenges." How do these affect the reader's response?

5. How does the article position the reader in relation to the issue—are they being invited to act, reflect, or admire?

4 Key Tips for Integrating Your Analysis

  • Connect the evidence types to their effectiveness in supporting the argument's credibility
  • Analyze how the portrayal of different groups serves the argument's persuasive purpose
  • Consider how cultural and diplomatic appeals create alignment with the reader
  • Reflect on how emotional appeals position the reader to respond to the issue
